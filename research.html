Foundational Methods

Molecular Simulation Methods Development

Before applying simulation to any material system, the Yingling group develops the underlying computational methods themselves — creating novel algorithms, force fields, and multiscale frameworks that make previously intractable problems solvable. Three landmark method families define this contribution:

Coarse-Grained Chemical Reaction (CGCR) Models

Prof. Yingling developed several coarse-grained chemical reaction models for both equilibrium and non-equilibrium processes, enabling simulation of complex chemical transformations at dramatically reduced computational cost. CGCR models are particularly powerful for large systems where full atomistic resolution is prohibitive — applied to laser ablation of organic materials, polymer degradation, and nucleic-acid-based nanostructure assembly.

Dissipative Particle Dynamics (DPD) for Soft Matter

The group developed multiscale DPD methodologies for predicting the stimulus-responsive behavior of soft and active materials. These methods enable accurate simulation of large-scale systems exhibiting stimuli-dependent behavior — covering polyelectrolytes, nucleic acids, elastin-like polypeptides (ELPs), reflectins, and block copolymer self-assembly in solution. DPD approaches correctly capture responsive transitions (LCST, coacervation, pH-response) that all-atom MD cannot access at relevant scales.

All-Atom Methods for Metal & Magnetic Nanoparticles

The Yingling group created and parameterized novel empirical force fields for ligand-functionalized metal and magnetic nanoparticles. This includes a force field that captures ligand affinity to nanoparticle surfaces, enables ligand stripping simulations, and — most distinctively — a new all-atom method to model magnetic nanoparticle alignment under external fields, enabling simulation of Zeeman alignment, dipolar assembly, helix formation, and rotational ordering in 2D and 3D structures.

  • CGCR: Applied to laser ablation (PMMA, organic solids), polymer space-environment degradation, RNA nanoparticle assembly
  • DPD / multiscale: Block copolymer morphology (Angewandte Chemie 2022), ELP coacervates (JPCB 2021), polyelectrolyte self-assembly; Foundations of Molecular Modeling book chapter 2021
  • Magnetic NP all-atom: Zeeman alignment & dipolar assembly method (JCTC 2022 cover); ligand stripping force field (ACS Nano 2023 cover; AMI 2025); silica shell on gold nanorods (Chem. Materials 2024)
  • Force field parameterization: Custom potentials for gold NPs, Fe₃O₄, graphene oxide (Data 2026), cellulose synthase, polyester/plasticizer systems for Eastman Chemical

Research Area 01

AI & Data Science for Materials Discovery

A core challenge in AI-driven materials science is handling small, sparse, fragmented, and heterogeneous datasets. The Yingling group develops advanced data fusion and integration workflows that combine multi-characterization experimental, simulation, and theoretical data to accelerate discovery.

Our work spans machine learning models for structure–property prediction, knowledge graph construction (BUILD-KG, INTEGRATE-KG, RL-CURATE-KG), large language model applications in materials informatics, and self-driving lab integration — all designed to work with the messy, multimodal datasets that real materials research produces.

  • Knowledge graphs & LLMs: BUILD-KG, INTEGRATE-KG, RL-CURATE-KG frameworks for heterogeneous data; Phosphorus Knowledge Hub (LLM-powered)
  • Small-data ML: Bayesian methods, uncertainty quantification, imputation strategies for sparse groundwater and materials datasets
  • ML + simulation fusion: Combining MD trajectories with scattering experiments and ML for structure resolution (ssDNA, nanoparticles)
  • Self-driving labs: Data integration perspectives for closed-loop autonomous materials discovery (APL Machine Learning 2025)
  • Materials informatics education: Graduate Certificate in Materials Informatics; DMSE723 course; MRS Advances perspective paper

Research Area 02

Nucleic Acid Nanotechnology & Nanoparticle Design

The Yingling group designs and characterizes RNA and DNA nanostructures using molecular modeling, enabling next-generation therapeutic applications. We developed models that accurately predict the structure and assembly of RNA/DNA-based nanostructures, overcoming major bottlenecks in gene delivery.

Our all-atom simulation techniques have transformed the study of nucleic acid–nanoparticle interactions, including a novel empirical force field capturing ligand affinity and allowing simulations of ligand stripping from gold and magnetic nanoparticle surfaces.

  • RNA nanoparticle design: Most stable siRNA-carrying RNA nanoparticles; DNA-driven condensates for immune modulation; dual-action nanoparticles targeting intracellular bacteria
  • DNA–nanoparticle interfaces: Gold nanoparticle design for dsRNA compaction; ligand-functionalized NP force fields; nucleic acid–surface interactions
  • Structure prediction: ssDNA structure resolution by ML + scattering fusion; computer-assisted RNA nanoparticle design workflows
  • Therapeutic applications: siRNA delivery, innate immune activation, silver nanocluster-mediated antibacterial targeting

Research Area 03

Soft, Responsive & Biomimetic Materials

We develop multiscale molecular methodologies for predicting stimulus-responsive behavior in soft materials. Our methods enable accurate predictions of responsive properties in polyelectrolytes, nucleic acids, elastin-like polypeptides (ELPs), and the squid-inspired protein reflectin, resolving key debates about responsiveness mechanisms.

Our cellulose research — supported by the DOE EFRC Center for Lignocellulose Structure and Formation (CLSF) — has produced landmark structural models of plant cellulose synthase complexes combining cryo-EM, crystallography, and all-atom simulation.

  • Elastin-like polypeptides: Phase separation, LCST behavior, coacervate structure; Science Advances 2019; J. Phys. Chem. B 2021
  • Reflectin proteins: Structure, self-assembly, and proton conduction; squid iridophore optics (Science 2026, under review); PNAS 2020
  • Cellulose & plant materials: Full-length CesA structure determination; Cellulose 2024 cover; Plant Direct 2021 top-cited
  • Block copolymers & amphiphiles: Morphology mapping for peptide-polymer amphiphiles; Angewandte Chemie 2022; ACS Nano 2024; Advanced Materials 2026
  • Biomimetic CO₂ capture: Carbonic anhydrase strategies; Trends in Biotechnology 2025

Research Area 04

Metal & Magnetic Nanoparticle Simulations

The Yingling group pioneered all-atom simulation techniques for ligand-functionalized metal and magnetic nanoparticles. We created and parameterized force fields for nanoparticle surfaces, enabling comprehensive atomic-level characterization of nucleic acid–nanoparticle and ligand–surface interactions.

We developed a novel method to model magnetic nanoparticle alignment under external fields, enabling simulation of 2D and 3D structures — helix formation, rotational alignment, dipolar assembly — in various solvent environments.

  • All-atom NP force fields: Novel empirical potentials for gold, silica-coated gold rods, iron oxide; ligand stripping simulations; JCTC 2022 cover
  • Magnetic nanoparticle assembly: Zeeman alignment, dipolar assembly, 2D/3D structure formation under external fields; phys.org news release
  • Ligand stripping: Solvent effects on colloidal nanoparticles; ACS Nano 2023 cover; Advanced Materials Interfaces 2025
  • ML-guided NP optimization: Machine learning and small-data optimization of silica shell morphology on gold nanorods; Chemistry of Materials 2024
  • Nanoparticle–bio interfaces: Biointerphases invited review; MRS Bulletin podcast feature

Research Area 05

AI for Sustainability & Environmental Science

Prof. Yingling leads AI and computational research within the NSF STC STEPS Center (Sustainable Phosphorus through Engineering and Policy Solutions), a national center tackling phosphorus sustainability — a critical global resource challenge. This work integrates AI, molecular modeling, and data science to address real-world environmental problems.

The group develops the PASTOR platform (Phosphorus AI for Sustainability, Traceability, and Off-grid Reasoning), the P1M Database (Phosphorus One Million), and ML tools for groundwater risk prediction — with direct policy and industrial applications.

  • STEPS Center (NSF STC): Phosphorus-binding protein simulations; hydrogel design for P removal; enzymatic transformation of P compounds; ES&T, Langmuir, Environ. Res. Comm. 2024–2026
  • PASTOR & P1M: AI decision-support for phosphorus sustainability; Phosphorus One Million database (Digital Discovery 2026 submitted)
  • Groundwater ML: Multiple imputation + ML for co-occurring inorganic chemicals; ES&T 2024; NSF press release; 8+ media outlets
  • Phosphorus Knowledge Hub: LLM-powered knowledge graph for phosphorus science; J. Environmental Management 2026
  • Water treatment: Nanoscale morphology effects in solvent-based desalination; ACS EST Water 2023; consulting for RTI International

Methodology

How we work: AI-augmented multiscale science

01

Multiscale Molecular Modeling

All-atom MD, coarse-grained models, DFT, and dissipative particle dynamics — choosing the right resolution for each scientific question, from electrons to microstructure.

02

AI & Machine Learning Integration

ML models trained on small, heterogeneous datasets; uncertainty quantification; Bayesian optimization; neural network potentials — embedded throughout the discovery pipeline.

03

Data Fusion & Knowledge Graphs

Combining experimental, simulation, and literature data via knowledge graph frameworks (BUILD-KG, INTEGRATE-KG) and LLM-powered pipelines to unlock hidden relationships.

04

Experiment-Simulation Synergy

Tight coupling with experimentalists — crystallography, cryo-EM, scattering, spectroscopy — to validate models and translate computational insights into real materials breakthroughs.
