Graduate & Undergraduate Courses

DMSE 723 2018 – Present

Data Science for Materials

Introduces materials scientists and engineers to data-driven methods: regression, classification, neural networks, and generative AI applied to structure–property–processing relationships. Students complete hands-on projects using real materials datasets.

Graduate Machine Learning Python Materials Informatics
MSE 591 2021 – Present

AI + Materials

A cutting-edge survey of how large language models, generative AI, and foundation models are transforming materials research — from autonomous labs and self-driving synthesis to AI-assisted literature review and hypothesis generation.

Graduate LLMs Generative AI Autonomous Labs
MSE 721/791 2009 – Present

Nanoscale Simulations & Molecular Modeling

Comprehensive treatment of atomistic and coarse-grained simulation methods: molecular dynamics, Monte Carlo, DPD, CGMD, and free energy techniques. Covers LAMMPS, GROMACS, NAMD workflows and force field parameterization with applications in soft matter and nanomaterials.

Graduate MD Simulations Coarse-Graining LAMMPS
AI Seminar 2020 – Present

AI-Driven Prototyping

A one-credit course on rapid AI-driven prototyping for scientists and engineers — from idea to working prototype using LLMs, generative models, and AI-assisted coding tools. Students build functional prototypes of research tools, data pipelines, and materials applications over the course of the semester.

1 Credit LLMs Prototyping AI Tools
MSE 290 2025 – Present

Materials Science Fiction

A one-credit undergraduate elective exploring how science fiction imagines materials of the future — invisibility cloaks, shape-memory alloys, self-healing structures — and connecting these visions to current research at the frontier of materials science.

1 Credit Undergraduate Interdisciplinary

Training & Outreach

Workshops, Short Courses & Outreach

Hands-On AI Tools Training — Developed & Delivered by Prof. Yingling
Spanning undergraduates, graduate students, faculty, and industry professionals across academia and industrial R&D

Prof. Yingling has developed and delivered hands-on AI tools training across every level of the scientific community — from first-year undergraduates encountering machine learning for the first time, to PhD students and postdocs integrating LLMs and generative AI into active research workflows, to faculty peers exploring AI adoption, and to R&D scientists at major industrial partners seeking practical skills in computational materials design.

This training spans short bootcamps, multi-day industry short courses, university workshops, and international hackathons. The common thread is practical fluency: participants leave with working knowledge of AI tools — not just conceptual awareness. Curricula are developed fresh for each audience, emphasizing real materials problems, live coding, and immediate applicability to participants' own work. Prof. Yingling's approach draws on her dual expertise in materials simulation and AI-driven discovery, bridging the gap between cutting-edge research methods and accessible instruction.
